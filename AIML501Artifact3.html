Introduction

Artifact configuration: AIML501 Artifact 3 — Generative AI LLM Training Process (created 2025). This artifact is formatted as a visual infographic presentation.

How to read the presentation: This infographic illustrates the complete training lifecycle of generative AI Large Language Models, from initial data collection through model deployment. It highlights key resources, costs, and specific examples from leading models like GPT-4, Claude, and LLaMA.

Overview

Training generative AI LLMs is a complex, resource-intensive process that requires massive datasets, substantial computational power, significant energy consumption, and extended time periods. This visual artifact breaks down each component to make the process accessible to varied audiences.

Key Steps in the Training Process

The complete lifecycle from data collection to model deployment

1. Data Collection

Purpose: Gather diverse, high-quality text data from multiple sources.

Sources: Web scraping, books, articles, code repositories, scientific papers, and curated datasets.

Scale: Models require terabytes to petabytes of raw text data.

2. Data Preprocessing

Purpose: Clean, filter, and format data for training.

Tasks: Deduplication, quality filtering, tokenization, and formatting into training sequences.

Output: Structured datasets ready for model ingestion.

3. Model Architecture Design

Purpose: Define the neural network structure and parameters.

Components: Transformer architecture, attention mechanisms, layer depth, and parameter count.

Decisions: Balance between model size, performance, and computational requirements.

4. Pre-training

Purpose: Learn general language patterns and knowledge from vast text corpora.

Method: Self-supervised learning using next-token prediction or masked language modeling.

Duration: Weeks to months of continuous training on thousands of GPUs.

5. Fine-tuning

Purpose: Adapt the model for specific tasks or improve alignment with human preferences.

Methods: Supervised fine-tuning, reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF), and instruction tuning.

Impact: Enhances model safety, accuracy, and task-specific performance.

6. Evaluation & Testing

Purpose: Assess model performance across various benchmarks and use cases.

Metrics: Accuracy, perplexity, safety evaluations, and human preference ratings.

Iteration: Results inform further fine-tuning and optimization cycles.

7. Deployment

Purpose: Make the trained model available for production use.

Infrastructure: Cloud deployment, API services, edge deployment, or on-premises hosting.

Ongoing: Continuous monitoring, updates, and maintenance to ensure performance and safety.

Primary Resources Involved in Training

The critical resources that drive the training process and their significance

Datasets

Scale: Terabytes to petabytes of text data

Sources: Web text, books, code, scientific literature, and curated datasets

Significance: Data quality and diversity directly impact model performance and capabilities. Larger, more diverse datasets enable better generalization.

Cost Factor: Data acquisition, storage, and preprocessing represent significant initial investment.

Computational Power

Scale: Thousands to tens of thousands of GPUs/TPUs

Hardware: NVIDIA A100/H100 GPUs, Google TPUs, or specialized AI chips

Significance: Determines training speed and feasibility. More powerful hardware enables larger models and faster iteration cycles.

Cost Factor: Hardware procurement or cloud compute costs represent the largest expense in training.

Energy Consumption

Scale: Hundreds to thousands of megawatt-hours (MWh)

Impact: Significant carbon footprint and operational costs

Significance: Energy efficiency is critical for sustainability. Training large models can consume energy equivalent to hundreds of households annually.

Cost Factor: Electricity costs contribute substantially to total training expenses and environmental impact.

Training Time

Scale: Weeks to months of continuous training

Factors: Model size, dataset size, hardware configuration, and optimization techniques

Significance: Longer training times increase costs and delay deployment. Efficient algorithms and hardware reduce time-to-market.

Cost Factor: Time directly correlates with compute and energy costs, making efficiency crucial.

Specific Model Examples

Training resources and costs for leading generative AI models

GPT-4 (OpenAI)

Parameters: ~1.7 trillion (estimated)

Training Data: ~13 trillion tokens (estimated)

Computational Power: ~2.15 × 10²⁵ FLOPs (estimated)

Training Time: Several months (estimated)

Energy Consumption: ~50,000 MWh (estimated)

Estimated Cost: $50-100 million (estimated)

Key Features: Multimodal capabilities, strong reasoning, extensive safety measures. Trained using a mixture of expert models (MoE) architecture.

Claude 3 (Anthropic)

Parameters: Multiple variants (Opus, Sonnet, Haiku) - Opus estimated at ~500B+ parameters

Training Data: Extensive web text, books, and curated datasets

Computational Power: Significant GPU cluster usage (details not fully disclosed)

Training Time: Several months of training and fine-tuning

Energy Consumption: Substantial (exact figures not disclosed)

Estimated Cost: Tens of millions of dollars (estimated)

Key Features: Strong safety alignment, constitutional AI training approach, excellent long-context understanding (up to 200K tokens).

LLaMA 2 (Meta)

Parameters: 7B, 13B, 34B, 70B variants

Training Data: ~2 trillion tokens from public sources

Computational Power: ~3.3 × 10²³ FLOPs (for 70B model)

Training Time: ~21 days (for 70B model on 2,048 A100 GPUs)

Energy Consumption: ~539 MWh (for 70B model, estimated)

Estimated Cost: ~$2-3 million (for 70B model, estimated)

Key Features: Open-source model, efficient training, strong performance-per-parameter ratio. Designed for research and commercial use.

Assignment Reflection

This artifact documents the training process of generative AI LLMs through a comprehensive visual presentation designed for varied audiences.

Creating this visual artifact on generative AI LLM training processes provided deep insights into the immense scale and complexity of modern AI development. Researching specific models like GPT-4, Claude, and LLaMA revealed the staggering resource requirements—from petabytes of data to millions of dollars in compute costs—that make large language model training one of the most resource-intensive endeavors in computing history.

The process of breaking down the training lifecycle into clear, digestible steps highlighted the importance of effective communication in technical domains. Each stage, from data collection to deployment, involves intricate decisions and trade-offs that significantly impact model performance and costs. Visualizing these relationships through flowcharts, charts, and comparative tables makes abstract concepts tangible for both technical and non-technical audiences.

Understanding the resource contributions—datasets, computational power, energy consumption, and time—illuminated how each factor directly correlates with model capabilities and training expenses. The comparison across different models demonstrated that while larger models often achieve better performance, efficiency improvements and architectural innovations (like Meta's LLaMA) can deliver competitive results with significantly lower resource requirements.
